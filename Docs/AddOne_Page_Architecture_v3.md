## GUIDING PRINCIPLES (apply to every section)

1. **The product and the founder tell the same story.** Viktor couldn't stick to anything → built a simple device → proved the principle on himself. Every section should feel like one continuous arc, not separate selling points.

2. **Simple idea, unexpected results.** The device looks almost too simple. That's the point. The simplicity IS the insight. Don't apologize for it — the whole message is that something this small can do something this significant.

3. **Honesty over polish.** This is Batch 1. 3D printed. Handmade. A passion project. That's not a weakness — it's the reason someone buys this instead of a $20 app. Frame it as supporting the project, not buying a product.

4. **Viktor's voice throughout.** First person where possible. "I realized..." not "Research shows..." The science comes through his experience — he read the books, didn't apply them, then built a device and felt the principles working on him directly.

5. **Community is woven in, not bolted on.** The friends, the group chat, the named units — these aren't testimonials. They're proof the idea works at a social level too.

6. **Personal touch is the product.** Your name on the back. A unit number. A video of it being printed. A photo of assembly. These are not add-ons — they ARE the product at this stage.

7. **Environment shapes you.** This is the through-line across every section — Viktor's story, the device's design, the community aspect — all comes back to "what you put in your environment changes what you do."

**LOCKED COPY:**

*December 4th, 2024.*

I decided to try the gym again. I'd lost count of how many times I'd started. But this time something was different — I wanted to track it somewhere physical. Not an app. I'd been slowly pulling away from apps, from the constant noise of the phone, the thousands of unread messages I'd stopped pretending to manage. I wanted something real. I just didn't know what yet.

Then I remembered something I'd seen years earlier on GitHub — the contribution grid. A simple visual: green squares filling up over time, showing exactly how active a project was. How alive. That image had stayed with me for years. It felt like what I needed.

What if something like that existed for a habit?

I opened ChatGPT. Then Claude. Then YouTube. I just kept asking questions — every question I didn't know how to ask, every concept I didn't understand. The answers came back immediately. I could learn something in an hour that would have taken weeks before.

There's a lot of debate about what AI means for the world. What I know is what it meant for me that evening: it meant I could build something I had no business building. That's not a small thing.

Parts arrived the next day.

---

*The project consumed me.*

Every evening after work. Every free hour. Late nights, early mornings, designing the 3D model, printing, failing, printing again. I have a mindset that things rarely work the first time — so I just kept going. Print. Test. Break. Print again.

A few weeks in, I had a working prototype. And one clear realization:

*I could make these for my friends.*

I forgot the last time I'd done something from the bottom of my heart. Something I thought was genuinely, incredibly cool — with no guarantee anyone else would see it that way.

I started making more.

---

*Christmas, 2024.*

I gave them out one by one. Each unit had a name engraved on the back. Not just "Founder's Edition V1" — their name. Made for them specifically.

I wrote them a message in our group chat:

*"I genuinely hope this device affects your life — not as a short burst of New Year enthusiasm, but as a quiet, long-term reminder of what you promised yourself."*

Then something I didn't plan happened.

They started posting photos of their screens in the chat. Asking each other: did you do it today? The accountability appeared on its own. Nobody organized it. It was just what happened when people could see each other's progress.

---

*A few months later.*

A friend who runs marathons — two kids, life constantly getting in the way — told me he hadn't skipped a week since Christmas.

Another friend received his device, excited to finally start a new habit. Before he could begin, it broke. He could have waited. Instead he took a piece of paper, drew the grid by hand, and started checking off days with colored pencils.

He didn't wait for the device. He just started.

That's the moment I understood: the idea was working on its own. The device was just the best version of it.

And that's when I knew this was bigger than a weekend project.
